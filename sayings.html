Sayings

When I was growing up my parents, particularly my Mother, and my grandparents would use a number of 'sayings' in there everyday conversation. Many of these sayings are not used today and so I thought I would jot down some that I recall and give, at least my interpretation of, their meaning.

Bob’s your uncleThis seems to be the British equivalent to "Hey presto!" or "Et voila!"
Budge up!Particularly if you are sitting, it means move along so that there is room for me.
ChinwagTo have a good natter (talk) with someone.
Don’t cast a clout till may is outIn this case, 'clout' is scrap of clothing and 'may' is the may flower (hawthorn). So it means, don't reduce the amount of clothing that you wear until the hawthorn bushes are in flower (late April, early May).
Don't count your chickens [before they hatch]Don't assume that everything will work out as planned until everything is done.
Let the cat out of the bagTell people or someone what was supposed to be a secret.
Put a spanner in the worksDo or say something that upsets what was intended.
Quids inA 'quid' is slang for one UK pound. So if someone was 'quids in' then they had benefited in some way.
Skew-wiffOut of alignment.
Spend a pennyIt was usually the case that to use a public toilet you had to put a penny coin (not used in the UK since 1971) in the lock to open the door. So the saying means going to the toilet.
Take the MickeyTake liberties at the expense of others.
White rabbitsIt was thought to be lucky to say this three times before saying anything else on the first day of the month.
